---
title: "Per-Speaker Audio Architecture"
---
Vexa captures audio differently depending on the meeting platform. This architectural difference directly affects transcription quality, speaker attribution accuracy, and how overlapping speech is handled.

## Platform Comparison

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Google Meet">
### Per-Speaker WebRTC Tracks

Google Meet exposes individual `<audio>` and `<video>` DOM elements per participant. Each element carries a separate WebRTC media stream.

**How it works:**
- The bot discovers one audio stream per participant via DOM media elements.
- Each stream contains **only that speaker's voice** — clean, single-voice audio.
- Speaker identity is resolved by correlating DOM speaking indicators with audio tracks using a **voting/locking** system: the bot observes which DOM tile shows the "speaking" indicator while audio arrives on a track, votes on the match, and permanently locks the identity after reaching a confidence threshold.

**Result:**
- No diarization needed — segments arrive pre-labeled with the speaker's name.
- High transcription accuracy — the model receives clean single-voice audio without cross-talk.
- Overlapping speech is handled naturally — each speaker's audio is on a separate track, so simultaneous speech does not interfere.
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Microsoft Teams">
### Single Mixed Audio Stream

Teams provides a single `RTCPeerConnection` stream containing all participants' audio mixed together.

**How it works:**
- The bot receives one combined audio stream via the RTCPeerConnection.
- The active speaker is detected by observing DOM elements that indicate who is currently speaking.
- Audio segments are **routed** to the detected speaker based on temporal overlap — when the DOM shows Alice is speaking and audio arrives, that segment is attributed to Alice.

**Result:**
- Speaker attribution depends on DOM active speaker detection accuracy.
- When two people speak simultaneously, the mixed audio is attributed to whoever the DOM indicates as the active speaker, which may miss the other speaker.
- Transcription quality is slightly lower for overlapping speech because the model receives mixed audio rather than clean single-voice input.
  </Tab>

  <Tab title="Zoom">
### SDK Raw Audio or PulseAudio Fallback

Zoom uses the native Meeting SDK when available, with a PulseAudio fallback for web-based capture.

**How it works:**
- **SDK mode** (primary): The Zoom Meeting SDK provides a raw audio callback with per-speaker data and participant metadata.
- **PulseAudio fallback**: When the SDK is unavailable, the bot captures system audio via PulseAudio — this is a single mixed stream similar to Teams.

**Result:**
- SDK mode gives per-speaker audio with accurate speaker identity from Zoom's participant API.
- PulseAudio fallback behaves like Teams — mixed audio with DOM-based speaker detection.

<Note>
Zoom support is **self-hosted only** — it requires your own Zoom Marketplace app and SDK binaries. Not available on the hosted service. A web-app mode (Playwright, no SDK) is in development.
</Note>
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Why This Matters

| Factor | Google Meet | Teams | Zoom (SDK) |
|--------|-----------|-------|------------|
| Audio per speaker | Separate tracks | Mixed stream | Separate (SDK) |
| Diarization needed | No | No (DOM-routed) | No (SDK metadata) |
| Overlapping speech | Both speakers captured cleanly | One speaker may be missed | Both captured (SDK) |
| Transcription accuracy | Highest | Good, lower for cross-talk | High (SDK) |
| Speaker attribution | Voting/locking on audio tracks | DOM active speaker detection | SDK participant API |

If your use case requires the highest possible transcription accuracy — especially for meetings with frequent cross-talk or overlapping speech — Google Meet will give the best results due to its per-speaker audio architecture.

## The Confirmation Buffer

Regardless of platform, all audio feeds into the same per-speaker pipeline:

1. **Track discovery** — audio streams are found (per-speaker on Meet, mixed on Teams).
2. **Speaker identity** — resolved via voting/locking (`speaker-identity.ts`).
3. **VAD filtering** — Silero VAD removes silence before transcription.
4. **Confirmation buffer** — the buffer resubmits audio every 2 seconds and publishes results in two stages:
   - **Draft** (`completed: false`) — published immediately on each transcription result (~2s latency to dashboard).
   - **Confirmed** (`completed: true`) — published after 2 consecutive matching transcriptions replace the draft.
   - **Hard cap** — 10-second wall-clock limit forces a flush regardless of confirmation status.
5. **Redis publish** — `XADD` to the transcription stream (persistence) and `PUBLISH` to the channel (real-time dashboard).

This two-stage approach balances low latency (drafts appear quickly) with accuracy (confirmed segments replace drafts once stable).
